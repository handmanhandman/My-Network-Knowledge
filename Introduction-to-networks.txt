## Types of Area Networks

We have different types of area networks, as they connect to each other:


### Local Area Network (LAN)

This type of network is for a small geographical area such as an office, home, building, and so on.


### Metropolitan Area Network (MAN)

This type of network is for cities and connects buildings, colleges, and so on, often with underground cables like fiber.
The fact is, the two types I explained to you are very fast!


### Wide Area Network (WAN)

The greatest example of this type of network is the Internet!
This type of network connects countries or continents with each other. However, compared to LAN and MAN, this type of network is slower.


What I mean by “slow” or “fast” is the time it takes for data to be sent and received through these types of networks.

We have different types of area networks, but the three I explained to you are the most important—trust me!

Okay, now I have explained what an area network is. It’s time to go back to the question you asked.

## Types of Network Connections

We have two different types of connections: **peer-to-peer** or **client-server**.

Now you might have a question: *“What the hell is a client or a server?”* LOL!

I got you. Think like this: you are in a coffee shop. The **client** is you, and the **server** is the barista taking your order.


In a **peer-to-peer network connection**, the two systems have full access to each other’s information. No one is there to take your order—it’s like coffee without a barista. You can do everything yourself! This type is good for small networks and has good speed for sending and receiving data.

On the other hand, in a **client-server network**, as I explained earlier, it is more secure. The server has one job: to take your orders. But this type of connection can be slower because there are many clients.

## Sending Data Across Long Distances

So far, I have explained to you local connections, like in LAN or MAN. We have physical media, but now is not the time to explain that; I will explain it later in Chapter 6.

Now, if we want to send data to another computer in another city or country, we use the big dog: **WAN**. In WAN, we have two protocols. The first one is:

**Multiprotocol Label Switching (MPLS)**
In this type of protocol, we create a secure path for data to travel through different routers in the WAN. By default, the data may take different paths to reach its destination, but with this protocol, we create a single, secure path for the data.

The other protocol is **Multipoint Generic Routing Encapsulation (MGRE)**. I don’t want to explain it to you—just search for it and learn about it yourself! That’s your job.
